# AI Ethics and Regulation: United States Perspective

## Overview

The United States approaches AI ethics and regulation through a multifaceted strategy emphasizing innovation, voluntary guidelines, and sector-specific oversight rather than comprehensive federal legislation. The cornerstone of this approach is Executive Order 14110, signed in October 2023, which established sweeping requirements for AI safety testing, transparency, and government oversight of powerful AI systems. This order mandated that developers of foundation models share red-team test results with the federal government and directed the National Institute of Standards and Technology (NIST) to develop the AI Risk Management Framework (AI RMF), a voluntary guide promoting trustworthy AI characteristics including validity, reliability, safety, security, accountability, transparency, explainability, privacy enhancement, and fairness with managed bias.

## Key Concerns

From the American perspective, AI ethics concerns center on national security, economic competitiveness, and protecting civil liberties while maintaining technological leadership. The U.S. prioritizes addressing AI-enabled threats to critical infrastructure, preventing AI-facilitated fraud and deception, managing workforce displacement, and ensuring AI systems do not undermine constitutional rights including privacy, free speech, and equal protection. There is significant emphasis on preventing foreign adversaries from exploiting AI for malicious purposes while simultaneously promoting American AI innovation and maintaining competitive advantage over nations like China. The U.S. also focuses on content authenticity through AI watermarking initiatives and addressing the concentration of AI power among a small number of technology companies.

## Policy Recommendations

The United States should strengthen its AI governance by expanding the AI RMF from voluntary guidelines to enforceable standards for high-risk AI applications, particularly in healthcare, criminal justice, employment, and financial services. Congress should enact comprehensive federal legislation that establishes clear liability frameworks for AI-caused harms, mandates transparency requirements for AI systems making consequential decisions, and creates a dedicated federal AI regulatory agency with enforcement authority. Internationally, the U.S. should lead in establishing democratic AI governance standards through multilateral agreements and continue promoting AI safety research through increased federal funding. Domestically, policymakers should incentivize AI ethics research, support workforce transition programs for AI-displaced workers, and ensure equitable access to AI benefits across all communities while maintaining American innovation competitiveness.
