# AI Ethics and Bias in Machine Learning

## Algorithmic Fairness and Its Foundations

Algorithmic fairness represents a fundamental challenge in contemporary machine learning systems, encompassing mathematical formalizations of equity that seek to ensure automated decision-making processes do not systematically disadvantage particular demographic groups. The academic discourse at Cambridge has increasingly focused on the tension between different fairness criteria—individual fairness, group fairness, and counterfactual fairness—which often prove mutually incompatible under realistic conditions (Chouldechova, 2017; Kleinberg et al., 2017). These mathematical frameworks, while providing rigorous foundations for bias detection, reveal an uncomfortable truth: fairness is not merely a technical property to be optimized but a normative concept requiring philosophical deliberation about societal values and distributive justice. The Cambridge approach emphasizes that algorithmic fairness must be situated within broader discussions of political philosophy, drawing upon Rawlsian principles of justice and Sen's capability approach to evaluate whether automated systems promote or undermine human flourishing across diverse populations.

## Bias Mitigation Strategies and Their Limitations

Contemporary bias mitigation strategies encompass three primary intervention points: pre-processing techniques that transform training data to reduce historical inequities, in-processing methods that incorporate fairness constraints directly into model optimization, and post-processing approaches that adjust model outputs to achieve demographic parity or equalized odds. However, Cambridge research has critically examined the limitations of these technical interventions, demonstrating that bias mitigation often involves unavoidable trade-offs between accuracy and fairness, and between different fairness criteria themselves (Mehrabi et al., 2021). Furthermore, the focus on statistical parity metrics may obscure deeper structural inequalities embedded within data-generating processes, potentially providing a veneer of fairness while perpetuating systemic discrimination. The Cambridge Centre for Human-Inspired AI advocates for a socio-technical approach to bias mitigation that combines technical interventions with organizational reforms, participatory design methodologies, and ongoing monitoring systems that engage affected communities in defining fairness objectives and evaluating algorithmic impacts. This approach recognizes that machine learning systems operate within complex social ecologies, and that effective bias mitigation requires not merely algorithmic adjustments but fundamental reconsiderations of how automated decision-making is deployed within institutional contexts.

## Ethical Frameworks for Responsible AI Development

Ethical frameworks for artificial intelligence must integrate consequentialist considerations of harm and benefit with deontological principles of rights and duties, while remaining attentive to virtue ethics traditions that emphasize the moral character of AI practitioners and institutions. The Cambridge Institute for Technology and Democracy has proposed a comprehensive ethical framework encompassing transparency, accountability, contestability, and human dignity as foundational principles for responsible AI development (Floridi et al., 2018; Whittlestone et al., 2019). Transparency requirements extend beyond mere explainability to encompass algorithmic auditing, data provenance documentation, and public deliberation about acceptable uses of automated decision-making systems. Accountability mechanisms must establish clear lines of responsibility for algorithmic harms, challenging the diffusion of responsibility that often characterizes complex AI supply chains involving multiple developers, deployers, and data providers. Contestability—the capacity for affected individuals to meaningfully challenge algorithmic decisions—requires not only procedural safeguards but substantive investment in digital literacy and access to technical expertise that enables genuine participation in algorithmic governance. Crucially, Cambridge scholarship emphasizes that ethical AI frameworks must grapple with questions of power and justice that transcend individual algorithmic systems, addressing how machine learning technologies reshape labor markets, democratic deliberation, and the distribution of epistemic authority in contemporary societies. The emerging field of machine learning ethics at Cambridge thus integrates technical rigor with philosophical depth and democratic engagement, recognizing that addressing bias and ensuring fairness requires not merely better algorithms but more just and equitable institutions for developing and deploying computational systems.
