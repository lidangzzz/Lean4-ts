# Theoretical Machine Learning

## Computational Learning Theory

Theoretical machine learning at Princeton encompasses the mathematical foundations underlying modern machine learning systems, with particular emphasis on computational learning theory, optimization theory, and the analysis of generalization bounds. The theoretical computer science group investigates fundamental questions about what is learnable, the sample complexity required for reliable learning, and the computational resources necessary to achieve specific learning objectives. Key areas include the Probably Approximately Correct (PAC) learning framework developed by Leslie Valiant, which provides rigorous bounds on the number of samples needed to learn a concept class with high confidence. Princeton researchers have made significant contributions to understanding the interplay between computational hardness and statistical learnability, particularly through the lens of average-case complexity and the analysis of learning problems that are computationally intractable despite being information-theoretically feasible. The theory group also explores online learning, reinforcement learning theory, and the foundations of adversarial robustness, developing frameworks for understanding when and why learning algorithms succeed or fail under distribution shift.

## Optimization in Machine Learning

The optimization component of theoretical machine learning focuses on understanding the convergence properties, implicit regularization, and landscape geometry of optimization algorithms used in training modern machine learning models. Princeton researchers have pioneered work on understanding why stochastic gradient descent (SGD) and its variants generalize well in practice despite being applied to non-convex objectives with numerous local minima. This includes fundamental work on the neural tangent kernel regime, which provides a theoretical framework for analyzing wide neural networks as linear models in feature space, and research on the role of optimization algorithms as implicit regularizers that bias solutions toward low-complexity hypotheses. The group investigates second-order optimization methods, variance reduction techniques, and the interplay between optimization dynamics and generalization, developing theoretical tools to explain phenomena such as double descent, grokking, and the benefits of overparameterization. Recent work addresses distributed and federated optimization, analyzing the trade-offs between communication efficiency, convergence speed, and privacy preservation.

## Generalization Bounds and Statistical Learning Theory

Generalization bounds form a cornerstone of theoretical machine learning research at Princeton, providing mathematical guarantees on the out-of-sample performance of learning algorithms based on their empirical performance on training data. The theory group develops refined complexity measures for hypothesis classes, including Rademacher complexity, covering numbers, and PAC-Bayes bounds, which provide tighter and more instance-dependent generalization guarantees than classical VC-dimension analysis. Researchers investigate the generalization properties of deep learning through multiple lenses: the role of margin-based bounds in explaining the success of neural networks, the connection between optimization trajectories and generalization, and the development of non-vacuous generalization bounds for modern architectures. The group also explores distribution-free and distribution-dependent learning theory, robust statistics in high dimensions, and the statistical foundations of self-supervised and transfer learning. Current research directions include understanding generalization in the interpolating regime, where models achieve zero training error yet continue to improve test performance, and developing theory for large language models and foundation models that challenges classical statistical learning paradigms.
