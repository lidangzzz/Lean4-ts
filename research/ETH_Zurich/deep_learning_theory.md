# Theoretical Foundations of Deep Learning

**ETH Zurich - Department of Computer Science**

## Neural Network Theory and Expressivity

The theoretical underpinnings of deep learning rest upon the interplay between network architecture, expressivity, and computational complexity. Universal approximation theorems establish that feedforward networks with sufficient width can approximate any continuous function on compact domains, yet these classical results do not explain why deep networks outperform shallow alternatives in practice. Recent work from ETH Zurich's Learning and Adaptive Systems group has demonstrated that depth provides exponential efficiency gains for representing hierarchical functions, with deep networks capable of expressing functions that would require exponentially many neurons in shallow architectures. The geometry of neural network parameter spaces reveals that the loss landscapes of over-parameterized networks contain numerous connected minima, and the implicit bias of gradient descent towards simple solutions provides a crucial regularization mechanism that enables generalization despite the vast capacity of modern architectures.

## Optimization in High-Dimensional Spaces

Understanding the optimization dynamics of gradient-based methods in deep learning remains one of the most challenging theoretical problems in machine learning. While classical optimization theory suggests that non-convex objectives should lead to poor local minima, empirical evidence demonstrates that stochastic gradient descent reliably finds good solutions in deep networks. ETH Zurich researchers have contributed significantly to explaining this phenomenon through the lens of neural tangent kernel theory, which shows that sufficiently wide networks behave as kernel methods in the infinite-width limit, with gradient descent exhibiting linear convergence. Furthermore, the interplay between learning rate schedules, batch size, and the sharpness of minima has revealed that large learning rates induce an implicit bias towards flat minima, which correlate with improved generalization. Recent theoretical advances also connect optimization to the geometry of the loss landscape through the study of mode connectivity, demonstrating that solutions found by SGD are connected through low-loss pathways, suggesting that the apparent complexity of high-dimensional loss surfaces may be more benign than previously assumed.

## Generalization and Statistical Learning Theory

The generalization capabilities of deep neural networks present a fundamental puzzle for classical statistical learning theory, as models with millions or billions of parameters successfully generalize despite dramatically exceeding traditional complexity bounds. ETH Zurich's theoretical machine learning research addresses this through several complementary frameworks: PAC-Bayes bounds provide tighter generalization guarantees by leveraging the stochastic nature of neural network predictions; information-theoretic approaches bound generalization error through the mutual information between data and learned parameters; and algorithmic stability analysis connects the dynamics of SGD to generalization through the sensitivity of the learning algorithm to individual training examples. These theoretical developments have revealed that effective capacity of deep networks is often much smaller than their nominal parameter count suggests, with architectural choices, optimization algorithms, and data augmentation all contributing to implicit regularization. The double descent phenomenon, where test error decreases, increases, and then decreases again as model capacity grows, has unified the apparent contradiction between classical overfitting predictions and modern deep learning practice, establishing a new theoretical paradigm for understanding generalization in the interpolation regime.
